## Design Context

### Users
Fyler is a desktop app for merging PDF files and images into a single PDF. It is intended for a very broad audience: people with little confidence using computers, casual home users, office workers, freelancers, students, and more experienced users who need fast control over document assembly.

The product must feel immediately approachable to someone with low technical confidence, while still supporting expert workflows such as page selection, reordering, preview, rotation, and export optimization. The core job is simple: gather files, review what will be included, make a few confident adjustments, and export without friction or confusion.

### Brand Personality
Fyler should feel simple, precise, and quietly capable.

The tone should reduce anxiety for less technical users. It should never feel toy-like, overly clever, or visually noisy. At the same time, it should communicate enough rigor and polish that more experienced users trust it for real document work.

Emotionally, the interface should create:
- clarity at first glance
- calm control during the workflow
- confidence at the moment of export

### Aesthetic Direction
The visual direction should be primarily clean and editorial, with a light premium layer.

That means:
- strong hierarchy
- restrained surfaces
- deliberate spacing
- clear typography
- subtle but polished emphasis on important actions

The current light/dark approach should remain in place. Accent color behavior should stay close to the current system rather than introducing a dramatic palette shift. The app should avoid generic SaaS dashboard aesthetics, decorative excess, and anything that feels trendy at the expense of usability.

### Design Principles
1. Make the primary workflow obvious within seconds.
2. Reduce cognitive load for first-time and low-confidence users through clarity, not oversimplification.
3. Preserve depth for experienced users through progressive disclosure and strong component consistency.
4. Use visual polish sparingly and intentionally: premium in finish, not flashy in expression.
5. Let layout, typography, and hierarchy do most of the work before adding color or motion.
